The Apache Software Foundation (ASF) is the nonprofit that quietly runs half the internet's infrastructure. Founded in 1999, it's the organization behind some of the most important open-source projects in tech — Kafka, Hadoop, Spark, Airflow, CloudStack, the HTTP Server that gave it its name, and hundreds more. It provides a governance model known as "the Apache Way," legal protection through the Apache License 2.0, and shared infrastructure for community-driven development.

Projects enter the ASF through an incubation process, where they prove community health and code quality before graduating to top-level project status. The foundation operates on the principle that no single company should control a project's destiny — a philosophy that has kept critical infrastructure vendor-neutral for over two decades. The Apache License 2.0 remains one of the most widely used permissive open-source licenses in existence.

**Why it matters:** The ASF created a sustainable model for open-source governance that protects both contributors and users. Its projects form the backbone of modern data infrastructure, and its licensing model made enterprise adoption of open source not just possible, but inevitable.
